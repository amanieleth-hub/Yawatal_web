About

Built for one job: telling you if a trip is worth taking.

Yawatal started from a simple, repeated frustration — accepting a request that looked fine on screen and realizing, halfway through the trip, that it wasn't.

Why it exists

Drivers see a fare. They rarely see a profit.

Every ride-hailing app in Addis Ababa shows a price before you accept — but none of them show what that price is actually worth once distance, time, and fuel are accounted for. Multiply that across several apps running on the same phone, and the mental math becomes impossible to do in the two or three seconds before a request expires.

Yawatal is built to close exactly that gap, and nothing more.

Who's behind it

An independent, single-developer product

Yawatal is designed, built, and operated by a solo developer based in Addis Ababa — iterating directly with driver feedback rather than through a large product team.

How the verdict is calculated

A deterministic engine, on purpose.

Yawatal's verdicts come from a fixed set of rules and thresholds — not a machine-learning model making a probabilistic guess.

ETB / minute & ETB / km

The two core figures behind every request, compared against clear thresholds.

Fuel cost

An estimated fuel cost for the trip distance is subtracted before a verdict is decided.

Fare tier & zone

Surge and mid-tier fares, and zone-specific multipliers, adjust the calculation the same way they'd adjust your own mental math.

We'd rather be upfront: Yawatal today is a transparent, rule-based calculator — not artificial intelligence. Forecasting features (like time-of-day demand patterns) are on the roadmap, and we'll say clearly if and when machine learning becomes part of how a verdict is produced.
What we optimize for

Three things Yawatal won't compromise on

Driver-first

Yawatal is built to serve the person driving, not the platforms it reads from.

Transparency

If we can't explain how a verdict was reached in plain language, it doesn't ship.

Respect for your screen

Yawatal reads what's already on screen to calculate a verdict — it never taps, types, or acts on your behalf.
